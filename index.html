Attribute Profiles in Madden 17¶

Brayden Kness & Gaurav Sharma

Introduction¶

The National Football League (NFL), is one of the most popular sporting leagues in the entire world, boasting over 100 million viewers during the Superbowl (Championship) alone. With its immense popularity, many people wish to participate in football, but after high school the only way to play is by being a collegiate or professional football player, both of which are incredibly different to do. Eventually Madden, a football video game, was created to allow people to simulate playing in football matches and play as real NFL teams.

A player in Madden is represented as a collection of attributes (i.e. overall rating, speed rating, strength rating, etc.), all of which are scored on a scale of 0-99. In this tutorial we will dive into the dataset of Madden 17 player ratings, looking to uncover similarities in the attribute profiles within each of the different positions, and build a model that can predict a players position, based on their attribute profile.

Getting Started¶

Before we can jump into any code we need to load a couple of libraries.

Pandas: If you are not familiar with Dataframes or Pandas, GeeksForGeeks has a great article outlining the use of Dataframes, and the Pandas documentation may be helpful.

Next we will use Matplotlib and Seaborn to create and display visualizations for our data.

Lastly, we will use Scikit-learn to build our prediction model.

The dataset we use in this tutorial can be found here. It contains the complete ratings of all attributes of players in the NFL. In addition to the attributes, it also has a players full name, team, and position.

def heat_plot(corr, ax, cmap=sns.light_palette("#2ecc71", as_cmap=True)):
    hm = sns.heatmap(corr, cmap=cmap, square=True, 
                     linewidths=2, linecolor='w', 
                     annot=False, ax=ax)
    
    bottom, top = hm.get_ylim()
    hm.set_ylim(bottom + 0.5, top - 0.5)
    return hm

Machine Learning and Analysis¶

Now that we have clearly shown that certain ratings correspond moreso to certain positions than others, lets build a model and train it on the data set, with the goal of predicting a player's position based on his ratings.

The first step is to split our data into features (independent variables) and labels (dependent variables). Since we are trying to predict a player's Position based on his attributes we will make our features the player's attributes and the labels the player's position.

In [182]:
# Features
X = madden.iloc[:, 5:]

# Labels
y = madden['Position'].values.ravel()

Next, in order to accurately train and test our model we will need to split the features and labels into training and testing sets. This task is easily accomplished using the train_test_split() method from the sklearn library. The parameter test_size is what percentage of the dataset will be used for testing. We chose 30 percent, but feel free to play around with other values.

In [183]:
X_train, X_test, y_train, y_test = train_test_split(X, y, test_size=0.3)

We now have everything we need to build, train, and test a regression model. Since we are trying to predict a categorical variable (i.e. position) we will use a Logisitic Regression model. If you are unfamiliar with Logistic Regression, here is a very nice video explaining the topic.

To build our model we will use the LogisticRegression class from thew sklearn library, and fit our training sets to the model.

In [184]:
regressor = LogisticRegression(solver='liblinear', multi_class='ovr')
regressor.fit(X_train, y_train);

With the model fit to our training sets, we can score the accuracy of the model by using the score() method, from the LogisiticRegression class, feeding in our testing sets.

In [185]:

To get a better feel for our model's accuracy, let's look at a confusion matrix of our predicted labels and true labels.

Before we do this, we will define another method that will let us easily compute and plot a confusion matrix, this will make it much easier to derive meaning from the matrix.

In [187]:
def plot_cm(actual, predicted, score,
            figsize=(12, 12), 
            cmap=sns.light_palette("#2ecc71", as_cmap=True)):
    
    labels = list(set(predicted))
    cm = metrics.confusion_matrix(actual, predicted, labels)
    
    plt.figure(figsize=figsize)
    ax = sns.heatmap(cm, annot=True, fmt="d", linewidths=.5, square=True, cbar=False, cmap=cmap)
    
    bottom, top = ax.get_ylim()
    ax.set_ylim(bottom + 0.5, top - 0.5)
    
    ax.set(xticklabels=labels)
    ax.set(yticklabels=labels)

    plt.ylabel('Actual Position');
    plt.xlabel('Predicted Position');

    all_sample_title = 'Accuracy Score: {0}'.format(score)
    plt.title(all_sample_title, size = 15);
    return ax
